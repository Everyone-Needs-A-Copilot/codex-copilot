# Presentation Narrative Arc: ENAC "Not a Pitch Deck" Structure

This is the story architecture for Everyone Needs A Copilot consulting presentations. Not a slide template. A narrative sequence that creates productive discomfort and leads to a clear ask.

---

## The Arc

### Stage 1: Cover (1 slide)
Name the real problem before they've said hello. The headline is the argument.

Never: "Our Services", "Introduction", "[Company Name] Capabilities"
Always: A complete thought that makes the client uncomfortable in a useful way.

Example: *"The Not-So-Unified Truth About Home Improvement"*

### Stage 2: Hello (1-2 slides)
Earn the right to what comes next. Why are you in the room? What have you seen?
Make it personal. Make it specific. Show you understand their world before you challenge it.
The Hello is not an agenda. It is not "thank you for having us."

### Stage 3: Unspoken Truths (2-3 slides)
Name what everyone in the room already knows but hasn't said out loud.
Label each: "UNSPOKEN TRUTH #1", "UNSPOKEN TRUTH #2", etc.

These are indictments of the status quo: stated plainly, using the client's own language.

Test: Would a senior leader feel seen and slightly uncomfortable simultaneously? If yes, correct.

### Stage 4: The Reframe (1 slide)
The single most important slide. One sentence that shifts the entire frame.
A reframe declares. It doesn't explain. It makes everything before it make sense.

Examples:
- *"You're Not in the Home Services Business Anymore"*
- *"The real competition isn't other retailers: it's the customer's own uncertainty"*

### Stage 5: The Questions Nobody's Asking (1-2 slides)
3-5 questions that expose the real problem. Questions, not statements.
Must come directly from the client's situation: not generic consulting questions.

### Stage 6: The Dilemma (1-2 slides)
Create productive tension. Make them feel the cost of staying where they are.
Make them feel the pull of where they could go. Do not resolve the tension here. Hold it.

### Stage 7: The Invitation (1 slide)
Introduce the solution as a relationship, not a product.
Not "Our Services." This is "Everyone Needs a Copilot."
The emotional shift from tension to possibility.

### Stage 8: Proof (2-5 slides)
Specific stories. Named client (or named type). Specific challenge. Specific outcome with numbers.
"We've done this before" is not proof. "$1 billion in two years" is proof.

### Stage 9: The Ask (1 slide)
Name it clearly: what it is, what it produces, what it costs, why now.
Never "we'd love to work with you." End with a specific next step.

---

## Sequence Rules

1. Every slide must advance the argument. If it doesn't, cut it.
2. Each slide creates a question the next slide answers.
3. Tension builds through stages 1-6. Relief arrives at 7-9.
4. Client's reality must appear by stage 3. If they don't recognize themselves: start over.
5. No agenda slides. No "About Us" as slide 2.

## The Headline Test

Read only the headlines in sequence. If they tell the full story: done.
If reading only headlines creates confusion: the arc is broken. Fix headlines first.
